Simplifi

February — April 2022

Based on a research on peoples experience living through the pandemic, we designed an application that offers opportunities towards improving the lives of those greatly affected by the pandemic.

The Problem

Due to the pandemic, many people’s financial situation has greatly changed around the world and has negatively impacted people's health, and ability to afford a healthier lifestyle in their daily lives.

Challenge

How can we help people affected by the pandemic both financially and overall health?

Our Solution

Simplifi is an app that would allow anyone with a smart phone to earn redeemable points for a bit of extra income by doing something beneficial towards their own lives such as fitness, wellness and saving on groceries.

The money for the rewards would come from partnered companies in return for promoting their services on the app. Users would be able to redeem their points for this money in the form of a gift card of choice or directly into their bank account.

Next Steps

Moving forward after the project, my next steps would be to continue to iterate on the visual identity and redo certain assets to really tighten it up the cohesiveness. I think I would also like to revisit the design of the navigation bar and consider a floating nav bar instead.

Most importantly I would have loved to do another user test and see how I may be able to iterate further on the revised user flow and design to optimize an even better experience for users.

Process

Project Statements

Based on quotes on how people felt during the pandemic, each group member created a project statement. On recommendation from our peers, due to our statements being within a similar context we decided to merge them into a new revised project statement that implemented aspect from each.

Polished Statement

What? Identify the System
“Simplifi” is an app that can allow the user to earn extra money directly from their smartphone.

Why? The Observation – Current State
Decreased hours and pay, especially during this pandemic, has greatly changed the financial situation for many people around the world. Due to this, many people have lost an incentive and motivation and or cannot afford to be healthier in their daily lives. Most people need to earn for a living, and being able to leverage an app that is simple enough to grow a little income by doing something useful like exercising or buying healthy groceries would benefit greatly.

What? The Proposition
Made for supplemental income, anyone is able to put cash in their pocket by performing simple tasks from the palm of their hand. These tasks could include rewarding with cash back for shopping, answering surveys, or selling items. Even walking could be an option too, as anything is possible. Money is incentive and could be obtained from exercises, grocery shopping, and wellness. The purpose of providing money is to encourage the user to provide a healthier lifestyle - physical health, mental health, and financial health.

For Whom?
This app would cater towards those in need of funds (ages 18 +) or to those who wish to earn extra money, but are already too preoccupied with work and do not have the time to hold a side job.

Requirements

With a solid concept down we began creating a requirements chart to begin fleshing out our task flows. Once we completed it we selected 5 requirements to develop and created a task flow for each one before moving onto visual design. As we moved forward we later phased out some requirements as they did not fit into the direction we were moving the app towards — e.g any messaging features.

Low-Fi Wireframes

With our visual design and fleshed out requirements, we started creating low fidelity wireframes taking into account our user’s and their needs, research, and peer feedback.

User Testing

After prototyping a hi-fidelity version off the lo-fidelity we moved onto user testing with a couple young adults. Whom were tasked with navigating and providing real time feedback on their experience. Which we later took and coded to continue editing the apps design for a better more fluid user experience. As well as solidifying our visual design altogether.

Insights

From our coded feedback here are our extracted insights which we based our edits off of.

Organization: Most of participants found the wording confusing — they didn't know where to go to complete tasks. The design lacked hierarchy which making navigation more difficult. Participants also found the point system confusing. - Some Participants liked that the app was straightforward and how everything was in the same place.

Redeem Rewards: The most confusing part of this task for 4/5 participants was understanding what redeeming points meant, and the idea that their reward points could turn into a cash payment. This could be fixed by adjusting the wording of the cash category. 1/5 participants felt that they would've liked to have seen double verification in terms of changing the points into actual cash.

Fitness: One of the easier tasks for most participants. Things to note, scanning linked card was well liked, but users were unable to choose it as an option (not prototyped). 1 participant felt there needed to be "link membership" feedback to let them know that the linking was successfully done. Another participant felt more categories/different organization would help users understand the different choices better.

Labelling: Most participants mentions better labelling conventions for sections like "stores", "categories" etc. Especially when it came to points, cash and bank related items. The naming of categories threw participants off when trying to complete tasks.  A participant was unsure of what the app was about.- The images helped participants navigate through the app

Design: Many participants enjoyed the simplistic design, and found the style consistent. There are a few adjustments to be made such as the search bar, making the home page more noticeable, and would like to have double verification, etc.

Wellness: Many participants thought that this flow was straightforward and easy to understand, and there was a good amount of information provided within event pages. However, purchasing a ticket was lacking steps between selecting a payment method and actually purchasing the ticket. A breakdown summary confirming the purchase would be beneficial towards the user experience and informing the user of their actions.

Prototyping: Participants reacted very positively towards the feedback of the loading screen, and side scrolling carousels to view content. Most participants either appreciated the request to use location or seemed indifferent, while 1/5 generally dislike allowing location tracking. There were hiccups when it came to selecting multiple options during registration ("select all that apply: physical health/nutritional health/wellness")

User Feedback: Participants had a positive reaction to the processing/ loading screen.

Things were easy to find for participants and they did not have to search too hard because the information was not overbearing. Participants also liked the map feature and how they could clearly see the points they collected.

Grocery: Overall the navigation was the most confusing aspect for participants in the beginning;  only 1/5 participants was able to locate the requested Loblaws coupon. However once past the first grocery screen and "sort by store" users had a much easier experience. Issues in terms of organization of and hierarchy seems to have contributed to this confusion.  An important note brought up by participant 2 is that they had no indication which coupons have been used.

Visual Design

During the pandemic many people were experiencing stressful situations so we took inspiration from the natural world as we wanted to create a relaxing experience.

Reflection

Target Audience

This project exposed me to important methods and techniques to the UI/UX design process such as requirements, task flows and user centered design. Through these my group and I were able to design around our target users by identifying what works for our users and key pain points.

User testing

As the project ran for about 3 months we had time to learn and conduct user testing. Allowing my group and I to make informed design decisions and collect data to back it up. This also help us design necessary iterations informed by our users.
